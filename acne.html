Overview

Patient Guide

Acne vulgaris is a common inflammatory disease of the pilosebaceous unit. It ranges from comedones and papules to pustules, nodules and post-inflammatory scarring. This guide explains causes, risk factors, types of acne, modern evidence-based treatments, prevention strategies and answers frequent patient questions.

Causes & Pathogenesis

  • Follicular hyperkeratinization: Excess keratin blocks the follicle entrance forming microcomedones.
  • Increased sebum production: Androgen-driven sebaceous gland activity creates an oily environment.
  • Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes): Bacterial colonization triggers inflammation.
  • Inflammatory response: Immune reactions lead to papules, pustules and nodules.
  • Genetic predisposition: Family history increases risk.

Types of acne (clinical)

  • Comedonal acne: Open (blackheads) and closed (whiteheads).
  • Inflammatory acne: Papules and pustules.
  • Nodulocystic acne: Deep nodules and cysts — more likely to scar.
  • Acne conglobata / fulminans: Severe inflammatory variants — rare and may require systemic therapy.

Treatment options (modern, evidence based)

Treatment is tailored by severity, scarring risk, patient preference and comorbidities.

Topical therapies

  • Topical retinoids (adapalene, tretinoin) — normalize follicular keratinization.
  • Benzoyl peroxide — antibacterial and helps prevent resistance when combined with antibiotics.
  • Topical antibiotics (clindamycin) — short courses, used with benzoyl peroxide.
  • Azelaic acid — anti-inflammatory and comedolytic; useful in pregnancy and sensitive skin.

Systemic therapies

  • Oral antibiotics (doxycycline, minocycline) — for inflammatory acne, short-term (3 months typically).
  • Oral isotretinoin — for severe/nodulocystic acne or scarring acne; requires monitoring for teratogenicity and labs.
  • Hormonal therapies (combined oral contraceptives, anti-androgens like spironolactone) — useful in females with hormonally influenced acne.

Procedural & adjunctive options

  • Comedone extraction, intralesional corticosteroid for stubborn nodules, chemical peels, fractional lasers and microneedling for scarring.

Prevention & skin care basics

  1. Use gentle, non-comedogenic cleansers twice daily — avoid harsh scrubs that worsen inflammation.
  2. Use oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturizers and sunscreens.
  3. Avoid picking or squeezing lesions to reduce scarring risk.
  4. Be cautious with cosmetic products — choose "non-comedogenic" labels.
  5. For females with persistent flares, consider gynecologic/endocrine evaluation for hormonal therapy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes acne?
Acne is caused by a combination of follicular plugging by keratin, increased sebum, bacterial colonization (Cutibacterium acnes) and inflammation. Hormones and genetics play a role.
Will acne go away on its own?
Mild acne sometimes improves with age; however, many patients benefit from early treatment to prevent scarring and prolonged disease.
Is diet important?
High glycemic index diets and dairy have been associated with acne in some studies. A balanced diet and attention to triggers may help; food effects vary by person.
How long until treatment works?
Topical treatments often take 6–12 weeks to show improvement. Oral antibiotics may show benefit in weeks, but full improvement may take months. Isotretinoin course is usually 4–6 months.
Is acne contagious?
No — acne is not contagious.
Can I use makeup?
Yes — choose non-comedogenic, oil-free products and remove makeup thoroughly before sleep.
What should women consider?
Hormonal acne may respond to combined oral contraceptives or anti-androgens (spironolactone). Discuss fertility plans before starting certain medications (e.g., isotretinoin is contraindicated in pregnancy).

Quick facts

  • Most common in adolescents but affects adults too.
  • Early treatment reduces risk of scarring.
  • Multimodal treatment often necessary.

Risk factors

When to see a dermatologist

If acne causes pain, nodules, scarring, psychological distress, or doesn't respond to OTC measures after 2–3 months.

Tools & Tests

Most acne is diagnosed clinically. Consider hormonal tests (androgens) in resistant female acne, pregnancy test prior to teratogenic drugs, and baseline labs before isotretinoin as per local protocols.
